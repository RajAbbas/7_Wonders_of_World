The History and Evolution of Seven Wonders

The Original Seven Wonders of The World

The Seven Wonders of the World is a list of remarkable man-made structures from ancient times. The original Seven Wonders of the World were the Great Pyramid of Giza, Hanging Gardens of Babylon, Temple of Artemis at Ephesus, Statue of Zeus at Olympia, Mausoleum at Halicarnassus, Colossus of Rhodes, and Lighthouse of Alexandria. However, all but the Great Pyramid of Giza have been lost to time. In 2007, a new Seven Wonders of the World list was created to showcase modern marvels of engineering and architecture.

Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal is one of the most iconic and recognizable buildings in the world. It is a mausoleum located in Agra, India, and was built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal. Construction of the Taj Mahal began in 1632 and took over 20 years to complete.

The design of the Taj Mahal is a blend of Indian, Persian, and Islamic architectural styles. It is made of white marble and is adorned with intricate carvings, calligraphy, and semi-precious stones. The main dome is surrounded by four smaller domes and four minarets, which are each over 130 feet tall. The symmetrical layout of the building is also a marvel of engineering and design.

The Taj Mahal is not only a beautiful building, but it is also a testament to the love that Shah Jahan had for his wife. Mumtaz Mahal died during childbirth, and it is said that Shah Jahan was inconsolable with grief. He commissioned the Taj Mahal to be built as a way to honor her memory and as a symbol of their love.

In addition to its architectural and cultural significance, the Taj Mahal is also an important site for tourism in India. It attracts millions of visitors every year and is considered one of the most popular tourist destinations in the world. The site was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and is often regarded as one of the Seven Wonders of the World.

Over the years, the Taj Mahal has faced many challenges, including pollution, weathering, and overcrowding. In response, the Indian government has implemented measures to protect and preserve the site, including limiting the number of daily visitors and restricting industrial activity in the area.

Colosseum

The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is an ancient amphitheater located in Rome, Italy. It was built in the 1st century AD and was used for gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, and public spectacles. Today, it is one of the most popular tourist attractions in Rome, drawing millions of visitors every year.

The Colosseum was built during the reign of Emperor Vespasian and completed in 80 AD by his son Titus. The amphitheater is made of concrete and stone, and its elliptical shape allowed for optimal visibility for the audience. The Colosseum was able to seat over 50,000 spectators and was known for its grandeur and innovative design.

The Colosseum was used for a variety of events, including gladiatorial contests, which were popular entertainment in ancient Rome. Gladiators were trained fighters who battled each other, wild animals, or condemned criminals to entertain the crowds. The Colosseum also hosted other events, such as mock sea battles, animal hunts, and reenactments of famous battles.

Over time, the Colosseum fell into disrepair and was used for other purposes, such as housing, workshops, and even a church. In the Middle Ages, it became a fortified castle and was used as a quarry for building materials. However, despite centuries of neglect and damage from earthquakes and other natural disasters, the Colosseum remains a testament to the grandeur and sophistication of ancient Roman engineering.

Today, the Colosseum is a popular tourist attraction and a symbol of Rome's rich cultural heritage. Visitors can tour the inside of the amphitheater, see the underground chambers where gladiators and animals were kept before the games, and learn about the history of the Colosseum through exhibits and audio tours. The Colosseum is also used as a venue for cultural events, such as concerts and theatrical performances.

Overall, the Colosseum is an impressive feat of engineering and a testament to the ancient Romans' fascination with entertainment and spectacle. Its historical and cultural significance has made it an enduring symbol of Rome and a must-see destination for travelers from around the world.

Machu Pichu

Machu Picchu is an ancient Incan city located in the Andes Mountains of Peru. It was built in the 15th century and is known for its stunning architecture and engineering. The site is considered one of the most important archaeological sites in the world and was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983.

The city of Machu Picchu is located at an elevation of 7,970 feet above sea level and covers an area of approximately 80,000 acres. It is believed to have been built by the Inca emperor Pachacuti in the 15th century as a royal estate. The site is divided into two main areas: the urban sector and the agricultural sector. The urban sector consists of temples, palaces, and public spaces, while the agricultural sector includes terraced fields for farming.

The architecture of Machu Picchu is characterized by its stone walls, which were built without the use of mortar. The stones were cut and shaped with remarkable precision, allowing them to fit together perfectly. The site also features a system of channels and aqueducts that were used to transport water throughout the city.

Machu Picchu was abandoned by the Inca people in the 16th century, and its existence was largely unknown to the outside world until it was rediscovered by American historian Hiram Bingham in 1911. Since then, the site has become a major tourist attraction, attracting millions of visitors from around the world every year.

In recent years, there has been concern about the impact of tourism on Machu Picchu. The high number of visitors has led to issues with overcrowding, erosion, and damage to the site's fragile ecosystem. To address these concerns, the Peruvian government has implemented a number of measures to limit the number of visitors and protect the site's cultural and natural heritage.

Despite these challenges, Machu Picchu remains a remarkable testament to the ingenuity and creativity of the Inca people. Its stunning architecture, breathtaking mountain views, and rich cultural history make it one of the most fascinating and awe-inspiring destinations in the world.

The city remained an important trading hub for centuries until it was eventually abandoned and lost to the outside world for centuries.

Petra remained largely unknown to the outside world until it was rediscovered in 1812 by Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt. Great Wall of China

The Great Wall of China is an ancient wall and fortification located in northern China. It is considered one of the most impressive architectural feats in human history, stretching over 13,000 miles (21,000 km) across mountains, deserts, and grasslands.

Construction of the wall began in the 7th century BC, during the Warring States period, when different Chinese states built walls to protect themselves from invaders. The first emperor of China, Qin Shi Huang, began work on what is now known as the Great Wall in the 3rd century BC. The wall was built to protect against raids and invasions by the nomadic tribes to the north.

Over the centuries, the wall was expanded and rebuilt by subsequent dynasties, including the Han, Ming, and Qing dynasties. The Ming dynasty, which ruled China from the 14th to the 17th century, is responsible for much of the wall we see today.

The Great Wall is made of different materials, depending on the section of the wall. In some areas, the wall is made of brick and stone, while in others, it is made of packed earth and wood. The wall is between 15 and 30 feet tall and up to 25 feet wide at the base, with watchtowers and fortifications built along its length.

Despite its impressive size and structure, the Great Wall was not impenetrable. Over the centuries, various invaders breached the wall, including the Mongols, who established the Yuan dynasty in the 13th century. In the 16th century, the Manchus breached the wall and established the Qing dynasty, which ruled China until 1912.

Today, the Great Wall of China is a popular tourist destination, attracting millions of visitors every year. It is considered one of the Seven Wonders of the World and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The wall has also been used as a backdrop for various films and television shows, including "Game of Thrones" and "Transformers: Age of Extinction." However, the wall is facing several challenges, including erosion, natural disasters, and tourism, which can cause damage to the structure. Conservation efforts are underway to preserve this ancient wonder for future generations to enjoy.
